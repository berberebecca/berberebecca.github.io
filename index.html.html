Why I would either be poor or dead in America

And no, it’s not because I am black or a woman

By Rebecca Habtemariam

What is the most valuable thing you are carrying with you right now (materialistically speaking)? A phone, wallet or laptop? In my case it’s two pen-looking injections with a very special liquid inside: Insulin.

Millions of Type One diabetics are dependent on the hormone insulin for their everyday survival. Due to a genetic defect their pancreas can’t produce the insulin anymore, which regulates the blood sugar. This is why they have to insert it themselves via an injection. In many cases type 2 diabetics rely on insulin as well, depending on the severity of their pancreas malfunction.

Insulin is not only valuable to diabetics. In fact, it is one of the most expensive liquids in the United States.

Even though the price has dropped a little bit in recent years, it stays on a high level. This development probably was not what the inventors of insulin intended - they sold their patent for the hormone for one Canadian Dollar each to the University of Toronto, to make it accessible to the wider public. The University needed help in making the hormone and therefore licensed the patent to Elly Lilly, now one of the biggest producers of insulin. But how are other countries managing their insulin prices?

In 2022, 100 Injection Units costed 22.8 Dollars in the USA.

Nowhere else is insulin that expensive.

The reason why insulin is so expensive in the US it’s because of it’s drug pricing system. Luckily, in 2023 congress decided that Medicaid or Medicare patients don’t need to pay more than 35$ for insulin anymore. But if you are not eligible for those programs, private health insurance is necessary. Most likely a so called high-deductible plan, which also comes with a higher cost. But even private insurance is not a guarantee for affordability, since some insurances don’t cover the costs for certain insulins.

This status quo leads many diabetics to dangerous actions, like skipping insulin doses, taking less insulin than needed or delaying buying insulin. According to a study from 2022, more than one million Americans ration their insulin. This practice led to the deaths of multiple diabetics in the last years.

America makes a well-handleable disease deadly again.
